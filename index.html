The Introduction to Paula Scher

How Paula Scher became “the master conjurer of the instantly familiar in graphic design”

Scher is a contemporary American artist and graphic designer. She is seen as one of the most influential graphic designers as she has been described as a “master conjurer of the instantly familiar” graphic design by The Pentagram. Paula Scher was born October 6th , 1948, in Virgina, but when she was a child, she moved around a lot, so she grew up between Philadelphia and Washington DC. This was due to her father's job as a photogrammetric engineer for the US Geological Survey. She would have seen the newest technologies and innovations from an early age. This is also where Scher found her love for art as her dad is and influence on her. Although Scher’s father and mother were against her going to an art school he later decided to let her attend. Her mother was from Brooklen New York and at an early age she would visit her relatives, driving through the Holland Tunnel which inspired her “The typography of the Holland Tunnel is emblazoned in my memory as this entryway to something big and powerful.” During high school she studied art and was known for making poster for big events at school like football games, that she would never attend she just did it because “I just thought I was good at art.” Scher grew up, she attended the Taylor school of Art, Elkins Park, Pennsylvania, where she completed her bachelor's degree of Fine arts. While she was there, she struggled her first semester until she picked up Graphic design were she “found out that graphic design wasn’t about that, it was about ideas.” career began in the 70s and early 80s when her eclectic approach to typography became highly influential. As a child Scher said she “I always knew that I wanted to be an artist.”

Scher’s Career

She then completed a move to New York in the 70s to begin her professional career where she worked as a layout artist at random House’s Children’s book division. She decided to leave this job and found another one in CBS records in the advertisement and promotion department. Two years later she joined a competing label, Atlantic Records, as an art director. This is where she “Started out as an illustrator” designed her first album cover. This led to her later returning to CBS records for eight years producing over 150 album covers annually. After eight years, in 1982, she resigned from CBS records. Her typography was influenced by Russian constructivism and without imitating its style she only made use of its vocabulary form.

Own Brand

In 1984, she decided to team up with a fellow graduate, Tyler Koppel, an editorial designer. They created a firm named Kopper and Scher. They ran it for seven years where she designed the iconic Swatch poster after the Swiss designer Herbert Matters work. Then in 1991, Koppel left the company to become a creative director for Esquire magazine. Scher also decided to leave as the company was crumbling after the recession. She left for Pentagram where she became phenomenally successful, she began in the design studio as partner of the company and eventually worked her way up the ladder to become the principal.

Teaching

While working for Pentagram, she began teaching at the School of Visual Arts and occasionally at prestigious art institutions including Yale and Tyler school of Art and Cooper Union.

Her Private Life

Scher married her husband Seymour Chwast' in 1973, but just five years after the marriage the couple divorced. In 1989 they remarried and have been together ever since. He was a huge influence on her work before they met as he created work with a clever blending of imagery and type.

Achievements

Scher has achieved many things in life. Some of these achievements are her first solo exhibition as a fine artist in 2006 at the Maya Stendhal gallery in New York, New York City. The collection was of maps, the lines represent political borders, “The land of the red rising sun” a representation of how we see Japan in 2004 after the Chuetsu earthquakes on October 23rd in Niigata, they were named Heisei 16 Niigata prefecture Chuetsu earthquake. The artwork is “full of mistakes, misspellings, and visual allusions to stereotypes of places such as South American, painted with hot colors and has two ovaries on the sides.” She sold every piece of artwork from the collection from the prices between $40,000 and $135,000. Due to the success of the exhibition, the gallery owner extended the show for an additional four weeks, and to accommodate the extension of the exhibition, they produced silk-screened map prints of cities, states and continents which were entitled “The world”. The following year, she created screen-prints of the New York City transit routes. She has also produced a limited-edition collection of maps of China that represent the rapid growth of the industry of the country as an attains superpower status from the world. Scher was influenced by her father to draw maps as that was his job when she was a young girl. Many of Scher’s designs have become synonymous with the New York City culture. Scher has collections of her work displayed at New York MoMA, Museum fur Gestaltung Zurich and the library of Congress in Washing DC. Scher is credited with the new identity creation of the Public Theater. She used a promotional graphic system that became highly influential in theatrical promotion for years to come. Scher has work presented at The Philadelphia Museum of Art, The Denver Art Museum, The Victoria and Albert Museum in London, The Bibliotheque nationale de France and in The Centre Georges Pompidou in Paris. In 1996, Scher’s widely imitated identity for the Public Theater won the coveted Beacon Award for integrated corporate design strategy due to its promotional graphic system. In 1998, Scher was named to the Art Directors Club Hall of Fame of Pentagram and later in 2000 she received the Chrysler Award for innovation in Design. Scher has been nominated for four Grammys in the past for Inspiring Designs. She was introduced to the national board of the American Institute of Graphic arts and became the president of the New York chapter from 1998 to 2000. In recent years, 2022, she received the D and AD Special Presidents Award. Scher has been an Alliance Grpahique Internationale member since 1993 and president of this organization from 2009 to 2012 where she retired from the organization's presidency.

Notable Work

Paula Scher has many notable pieces of work like the “Bring in Da noise, Bring in Da Funk” for the Public Theatre in 1995, the “Bloomberg signage”, environmental graphics and media installations, 2005, the National Theatre poster in 2013 and the “War/Love” for the Public Theatre 2016-2017 season campaign in 2016. More recently in 2017, Quad cinema's identity, like what she had done for the Public Theatre.

Scher's Book: Maps

Her maps show a different side to her creative talents, a “Dense, colourful and displaying a wealth of wordy detail, they conjure up a powerful sense of place that manages to suggest both popular opinion and personal significance.” Scher's maps are hand printed screen prints that are described as “beautiful renditions of her original painted maps.” collection of maps was created as a collab with Andy Warhol’s printer Alexander Heinrici. After all the maps were released, Scher created a book called “Maps” she opens the book with an essay about her father's influence on her and her idea to do paintings of maps. This was due to his job and the impact it had on the US Geological Survey. The book went on to then show the real-life size paintings as the paintings are posters that fold out.

What is Scher doing today?

Today Scher is still working with graphic design. She is currently still working with Quad Cinema. Quad cinema has four theaters, and her team has completed the identity rebrand, environmental graphics, awning, digital media, architectural development, and interior design. Scher and her team have also started working on “the identity for the New Jersey Performing Arts Centre.” This for pier 55, for Pasadena playhouse and their five parks, they are going to rebrand the architecture of the parks to make them more environmentally safe while also keeping them up to date.

My Conclusion

In conclusion Paula Scher has become a “master conjurer of the instantly familiar in graphic design” due to her hard work and the opportunities she created for herself from the first job she got in New York right down to her company's failure and her move for it to Pentagram. She made herself known with her amazing artwork and her ability to introduce new influences while keeping the artwork focused on the main point. She never overpowered the focus. “Now with a fifty-year career and 300 design awards to her name, Paula is undoubtedly an industry icon and may just be the most in-demand graphic design teacher around.”
